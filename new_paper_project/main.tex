\abstract{
% Multi-Objective Reinforcement Learning (MORL) presents significant challenges when dealing with conflicting objectives that possess vastly different magnitudes (e.g., latency in milliseconds vs. energy in Joules). Traditional scalarization techniques, such as Linear or Chebyshev scalarization, often suffer from gradient instability or require extensive manual tuning of weights. This paper proposes a domain-agnostic framework, \textit{Fuzzy-MCDM Reward Shaping}, which integrates Fuzzy TOPSIS into the Reinforcement Learning loop to dynamically normalize and scalarize multi-dimensional reward vectors. By mapping the state-action evaluation to a closeness coefficient relative to an ideal solution, we provide a bounded and stable reward signal. To validate this general framework, we apply it to a complex, non-linear Heterogeneous Edge Computing problem. Experimental results demonstrate that our approach outperforms traditional scalarization methods, achieving stable convergence and reducing energy consumption by up to 90\% in the validation scenario, proving its efficacy as a robust solver for disparately scaled multi-objective problems.
}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec_introduction}
The rapid and continuous evolution of communication technologies, such as \textit{5G New Radio} (NR) and \textit{Vehicle-to-Everything} (V2X), has driven the increasing sophistication of vehicular applications that require high-performance real-time execution. Modern vehicles are equipped with multiple sensors, communication interfaces, and processing units within their \textit{On-Board Units} (OBUs), enabling the collection, sharing, and massive processing of data. However, the increasing complexity of these services frequently exceeds the intrinsic computational capacity of embedded devices.

A straightforward approach to mitigating this limitation would be to expand OBU hardware capabilities. Nevertheless, the current technological transition period results in heterogeneous scenarios in which devices with distinct computational capacities~\cite{luo2024} and diverse communication technologies~\cite{zheng2015} coexist. This heterogeneity poses a fundamental challenge to the scalability of vehicular applications in \textit{Smart Cities}~\cite{luo2024}, particularly under highly dynamic conditions.

Computational limitations can be mitigated through the adoption of \textit{Vehicular Edge Computing} (VEC). This architecture enables \textit{User Equipment} (UE) devices to offload computationally intensive tasks to edge servers, where they can be processed with lower latency due to their proximity, often at road intersections. Applications that significantly benefit from this paradigm include those focused on autonomous driving, which impose stringent latency requirements~\cite{chao2025}.

Managing and orchestrating such an environment is inherently complex. Developing a centralized solution capable of handling a growing and variable number of devices, which often have conflicting objectives, represents a significant challenge. For instance, an autonomous electric vehicle aims to minimize its battery consumption while meeting the \textit{Quality of Service} (QoS) requirements of its applications. Furthermore, from a system-level stability perspective, overloading a VEC server is undesirable.

The integration of vehicle mobility into cellular and wireless networks introduces a highly dynamic environment characterized by \textbf{uncertainties}. Unlike static systems, the statistical properties of these networks evolve over time, leading to abrupt temporal variations in workload and channel conditions, commonly referred to as \textit{drifts}. Beyond these physical and environmental challenges, real-world scenarios must also consider the decentralized nature of UEs. When multiple UEs compete for limited resources, their independently optimized policies may trigger a ``herd effect,'' a phenomenon in which users simultaneously converge toward the same resources, causing severe congestion and significantly degrading QoS.

Traditional \textit{Reinforcement Learning} (RL) approaches often prove insufficient in such scenarios, as they typically rely on reactive strategies that struggle to cope with the rapid obsolescence of learned policies induced by environmental drifts. Although RL has achieved remarkable success in single-objective settings, real-world vehicular environments are inherently multi-agent and multi-objective. Agents must simultaneously optimize conflicting criteria while interacting with other adaptive entities, rendering the learning dynamics non-stationary. While \textit{Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning} (MARL) captures inter-agent strategic behavior and \textit{Multi-Objective Reinforcement Learning} (MORL) addresses objective trade-offs, combining both paradigms is crucial for modeling realistic VEC scenarios.

\section{The Proposed Framework}\label{sec_framework}
This section describes the \textit{Fuzzy-MCDM Reward Shaping} framework in a general context, applicable to any MOMDP (Multi-Objective Markov Decision Process).

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
Consider a MOMDP defined by $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{P}, \vec{\mathbf{r}}, \gamma)$, where $\vec{\mathbf{r}}(s, a) \in \mathbb{R}^k$ is a vector of $k$ conflicting objectives. The goal is to find a policy $\pi$ that optimizes the scalarized return.

\subsection{The Normalization Challenge}
In many domains, $\vec{\mathbf{r}} = [r_1, r_2, \dots, r_k]$ where $r_1 \approx 10^{-3}$ and $r_2 \approx 10^4$. A naive scalarization $R = w_1 r_1 + w_2 r_2$ results in optimization biased entirely towards $r_2$ unless $w_1 \gg w_2$. Determining these weights dynamically is an open research problem.

\subsection{Fuzzy TOPSIS as a Reward Layer}
We introduce a reward processing layer. Instead of feeding $\vec{\mathbf{r}}$ directly to the agent, we compute the similarity to an ideal solution.
Let $D$ be the decision matrix of possible actions in state $s$. We model the outcomes as Triangular Fuzzy Numbers (TFNs) to handle the epistemic uncertainty of the environment (e.g., stochastic transitions).

The framework calculates the Euclidean distance of the current action's outcome to the Fuzzy Positive Ideal Solution (FPIS, $A^+$) and the Fuzzy Negative Ideal Solution (FNIS, $A^-$). The shaped reward $R_{shaped}$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
    R_{shaped}(s, a) = \eta \cdot \frac{d(a, A^-)}{d(a, A^+) + d(a, A^-)}
\end{equation}
Crucially, $R_{shaped} \in [0, \eta]$ regardless of whether the original objectives were in microseconds or gigajoules. This property guarantees gradient stability for Deep RL algorithms.

\section{Case Study: Heterogeneous Computing}\label{sec_case_study}
To empirically validate the proposed framework, we selected a domain known for extreme scale disparity: Edge Computing with Dynamic Voltage and Frequency Scaling (DVFS).

\subsection{Environment Setup}
The environment consists of heterogeneous servers where execution time ($T$) and Energy ($E$) follow the non-linear relationship $E \propto T^{-2}$ (since $P \propto f^3$ and $T \propto f^{-1}$).
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Objective 1 (Latency):} Magnitude $\approx 10^0$ seconds.
    \item \textbf{Objective 2 (Energy):} Magnitude $\approx 10^5$ Joules.
\end{itemize}
This 5-order-of-magnitude difference makes it an ideal stress test for our scalarization-free framework.

\subsection{Baselines}
We compare our method against:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Standard RL (Chebyshev):} A state-of-the-art MORL scalarization technique.
    \item \textbf{Heuristics:} Random, Round-Robin, and Greedy (Least-Load).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Policy Convergence}
The entropy analysis (Fig. \ref{fig_results}, bottom) serves as a proxy for the framework's ability to produce learnable signals. The proposed method's entropy decayed smoothly to $\approx 0.1$, indicating that the Fuzzy TOPSIS signal was consistent enough for the Actor to form a confident policy. The traditional MORL baselines fluctuated, suggesting that the scalarized reward signal was too noisy or contradictory for the neural network updates.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec_conclusion}
This paper presented a generalized framework for Multi-Objective Reinforcement Learning that utilizes Fuzzy TOPSIS for dynamic reward shaping. By decoupling the magnitude of the objectives from the reward signal, we solved the scale disparity problem inherent in many engineering domains.
While we validated the method in a Task Offloading scenario, achieving significantly superior energy efficiency, the underlying methodology is domain-agnostic. It provides a robust alternative to manual weight tuning in any system where objectives are conflicting and heterogeneously scaled.
